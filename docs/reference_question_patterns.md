# Reference: real deposition question patterns

This document captures a worked example of the first 20 questions of a civil
deposition, with the tactic, form risk, and ideal witness response annotated for
each. It is **reference material** for prompt engineering — it shows the level
of sophistication the AI examiner should aspire to in BACKGROUND / FOUNDATION
questioning. Drawn from a Marie-Louise Cossette / Class AZ commercial driver
matter; the *patterns* transfer to any civil deposition (slip-and-fall,
employment, contract, etc.).

> Witness-prep takeaway: 80% of damaging admissions happen in the first 20
> questions, before the witness mentally "switches on". The opening is where
> sloppy answers get cemented as record evidence.

---

## Q1 — Direct opener
- **Counsel:** "Please state your full legal name for the record."
- **Type:** Narrowly closed, neutral
- **Tactic:** Standard opener. Sets pacing baseline. Witness expects this.
- **Form risk:** Volunteering. Some witnesses say "Marie-Louise Cossette, I'm 47 years old and I drive truck" — three answers to one question.
- **Ideal form:** "Marie-Louise Cossette." Stop.

## Q2 — Absolute name lock-in
- **Counsel:** "And you have never gone by any other name — correct?"
- **Type:** Closed leading, absolute
- **Tactic:** "Never" is a trap word. Maiden name, nicknames on tax returns, French-vs-English variants ("Marie Louis" appears in some court documents as a typo — counsel may exploit any variant).
- **Form risk:** Saying "no, never" when she has used another name. Or volunteering an explanation.
- **Ideal form:** If true, "Correct." If not, "I have also gone by [X]." Don't elaborate.

## Q3 — Current address
- **Counsel:** "What is your current residential address?"
- **Type:** Narrowly closed
- **Tactic:** Establishes record-of-address. Also tests whether she gives just an address or volunteers about who lives with her, how long she's been there, etc.
- **Form risk:** Volunteering household details, length of residence, prior addresses.
- **Ideal form:** Address. Stop.

## Q4 — Casual lock-in on residence duration
- **Counsel:** "And you've lived at that address since — what — the late nineties?"
- **Type:** Closed leading with offered range
- **Tactic:** "What — the late nineties?" mimics conversational tone, lulls witness into picking a number that may not match prior statements or production. Range-offering is the standard "pick one" trap.
- **Form risk:** Guessing. Picking from the offered range to seem cooperative.
- **Ideal form:** "I'd have to check the exact date. It has been many years."

## Q5 — Marital status
- **Counsel:** "Are you currently married?"
- **Type:** Narrowly closed
- **Tactic:** Sets up future questions about whom she has discussed the matter with (spouse is a fact witness, no privilege in Ontario for spousal communications in civil discovery).
- **Form risk:** Volunteering spouse's occupation, length of marriage.
- **Ideal form:** "Yes." or "No." Stop.

## Q6 — Date of birth
- **Counsel:** "Your date of birth?"
- **Type:** Narrowly closed
- **Tactic:** Establishes age. Sets up "experienced driver" math later (Q13–Q14). Also relevant to pre-existing condition defence (Defence ¶10) — older worker, more likely to have a prior orthopedic history.
- **Form risk:** Volunteering health information ("I just turned 50, but I'm in great shape").
- **Ideal form:** Date. Stop.

## Q7 — Education — open
- **Counsel:** "Tell me about your education."
- **Type:** Open
- **Tactic:** Opposite of Q1–Q6's narrow style. Aggressive counsel switches to open questions deliberately to invite volunteering. Multi-minute education answers can include schools she didn't graduate from, certifications that lapsed — every detail is a fact opposing counsel will mine.
- **Form risk:** Multi-minute educational narrative. Naming institutions she'd have to back up with records.
- **Ideal form:** Brief factual summary — highest level completed, year, institution. Stop.

## Q8 — Language preference
- **Counsel:** "Are you proceeding in English today, or would you prefer to be examined in French?"
- **Type:** Open binary
- **Tactic:** "Marie-Louise" is a francophone name; in Ontario this is a procedural question, but it also tests how she responds to a question that has no trap. Baseline question. Pacing / tone check.
- **Form risk:** Over-explaining ("I'm comfortable in both" — what counsel needs is a one-word answer).
- **Ideal form:** "English." Stop.

## Q9 — Prior testimony experience
- **Counsel:** "Have you ever been examined for discovery before — in any matter?"
- **Type:** Closed, absolute ("any matter")
- **Tactic:** "Any matter" is broad. Captures prior WSIB hearings, prior small claims, prior family matters. Each prior testimony is a potential prior inconsistent statement.
- **Form risk:** Forgetting a long-ago WSIB or small-claims appearance, then having to "remember" later.
- **Ideal form:** Honest factual answer. If unsure, "I don't recall any other examinations, but I'd want to think about it before answering definitively."

## Q10 — Health today
- **Counsel:** "Are you currently taking any medication that would affect your ability to give clear and complete answers today?"
- **Type:** Closed, formulaic
- **Tactic:** Standard procedural protection for the record. But also a subtle setup — any "yes" opens medical-records production. Any "no" is a representation that her answers today are her best memory.
- **Form risk:** Volunteering medication names or dosages. Discussing pain.
- **Ideal form:** "No." If yes: "I take [generic class], and I don't believe it affects my answers." No more.

## Q11 — The understanding-questions formality
- **Counsel:** "And you understand that if a question is unclear, you should ask me to repeat or rephrase it — rather than guess at what I mean?"
- **Type:** Closed leading
- **Tactic:** Counsel is creating record-discipline cover. Once she says yes, any later vague answer becomes "but you agreed at the start that you'd ask if a question was unclear."
- **Form risk:** Treating this as throat-clearing and then guessing on later questions anyway.
- **Ideal form:** "Yes." Then USE the protection — actually ask for clarification later when needed.

## Q12 — Driving credentials — narrowly closed
- **Counsel:** "You hold a Class AZ commercial driver's licence — correct?"
- **Type:** Closed leading with embedded specific
- **Tactic:** "Class AZ" is the Ontario commercial licence for tractor-trailers. It's also the brand name of her company. The question quietly conflates the two — locks in active commercial driver status as of today.
- **Form risk:** Saying "yes" without noticing the present-tense lock-in (i.e., she's still licensed despite the alleged income loss).
- **Ideal form:** "Yes, I hold a Class AZ licence." Confirm only what's true today.

## Q13 — Date of licence
- **Counsel:** "When did you first obtain your Class AZ licence?"
- **Type:** Narrowly closed
- **Tactic:** Date specifics. Math will be used later to compute her years of experience as of the January 16, 2002 fall — and to establish she "knew" winter driving conditions (Defence ¶9(b) and (d)).
- **Form risk:** Guessing on the year. Most witnesses can't pin a specific date this far back.
- **Ideal form:** "I'd have to check the exact date. Approximately [year], but I'd want to verify."

## Q14 — Compound experience trap
- **Counsel:** "And by January of 2002, you had been driving tractor-trailers commercially for — what — ten, fifteen years?"
- **Type:** Compound, leading, with offered range
- **Tactic:** Same range-offering trap as Q4 but with higher stakes. A high number = experienced driver who "should have known" about winter conditions (contributory negligence). A low number = inexperience the defence will use elsewhere.
- **Form risk:** Picking from the offered numbers. Volunteering employers from prior years.
- **Ideal form:** "I'd been driving commercially for approximately [her best estimate] years." Don't accept either offered number unless it's accurate.

## Q15 — Current employer — innocent-sounding lock-in
- **Counsel:** "And as of today, who is your employer?"
- **Type:** Narrowly closed
- **Tactic:** If she says "AZ Tractor Transport" — counsel begins to lock in objective 1 (Cossette IS AZ). If she says "myself" or "I'm self-employed" — counsel still gets the lock-in via the obvious follow-up.
- **Form risk:** Long-winded explanation of corporate structure. Volunteering ownership percentage.
- **Ideal form:** "AZ Tractor Transport." Or: "I work through 1460930 Ontario Limited, doing business as AZ Tractor Transport." Stop.

## Q16 — Corporate role compound
- **Counsel:** "You are the sole director, sole officer, and sole shareholder of 1460930 Ontario Limited — yes?"
- **Type:** Compound, leading
- **Tactic:** Objective 1 kill shot. Three facts in one. Cossette pleaded herself as Officer, Director and Employee in Claim ¶2 — this question lets opposing counsel cement that as record evidence.
- **Form risk:** Saying "yes" to all three at once without noticing she's confirmed every element of "Cossette = AZ".
- **Ideal form:** Truthful answer, broken apart if needed: "I'm the sole director and officer. I'm also the sole shareholder."

## Q17 — Date of incorporation — authoritative recital
- **Counsel:** "And that company — 1460930 Ontario Limited — was incorporated on [date counsel reads from corporate search]. Correct?"
- **Type:** Closed leading, with authoritative-recital framing
- **Tactic:** Reading from "the corporate search" implies counsel has the answer. Most witnesses confirm rather than say "I don't recall." If the date is wrong, she's locked into an error.
- **Form risk:** Confirming a date she can't actually verify in the moment.
- **Ideal form:** "If that's what the search shows, I won't dispute it — I don't have the document in front of me."

## Q18 — Friendly-sounding why-incorporated trap
- **Counsel:** "And you incorporated for the usual reasons — tax efficiency, liability protection, that sort of thing?"
- **Type:** Closed leading, with "everyone does this" framing
- **Tactic:** The "usual reasons" framing makes the question feel like a polite acknowledgement. But if she agrees to "liability protection", counsel later argues she can't have it both ways — sole shareholder AND personally injured for purposes of a personal-injury claim about a workplace accident.
- **Form risk:** Agreeing to a characterization to seem reasonable.
- **Ideal form:** "I incorporated on the advice of my accountant. I couldn't speak to the specific reasons."

## Q19 — Prior workplace incidents — absolute
- **Counsel:** "Prior to January 16, 2002, you had never been involved in any workplace accident or incident — correct?"
- **Type:** Closed leading, absolute, embedded date
- **Tactic:** "Never" + "any." This is a fishing question for pre-existing condition (Defence ¶10) and for prior WSIB claims (objective 13). If she says yes and there was a prior incident, counsel impeaches with WSIB records.
- **Form risk:** A flat "never" when she has had any minor incident — even a near-miss with no claim.
- **Ideal form:** "I don't recall any prior workplace accidents. If you have a specific incident in mind, I'd want to be shown what you're referring to."

## Q20 — Prior insurance / benefit claims — sweep
- **Counsel:** "And you've never filed any prior insurance claim, WSIB claim, or disability claim of any kind — for any reason — at any time?"
- **Type:** Closed leading, multi-stacked absolute
- **Tactic:** Three "any"s and a "never." This is a perjury-trap-tier question — almost no adult has truly never filed any claim. Counsel wants either a flat "never" (impeachable) or a hedge (looks evasive). Either way she's worse off.
- **Form risk:** Saying "no" reflexively when she's filed at least one minor claim in her life.
- **Ideal form:** "I don't recall any prior claims off the top of my head. If you have specific claims in mind, I'd want to be shown them before I answer definitively."

## Transfer notes for the slip-and-fall scenario (or any civil deposition)

The same trap patterns map across cases. For a slip-and-fall plaintiff:
- Q4-style range-offering on "and you'd been a customer of Shop A since — what — the early 2010s?" sets up Defence ¶3 (awareness of risk).
- Q14-style compound: "And on the morning in question you weren't wearing winter boots, you weren't using a cane or other walking aid, and you weren't accompanied by anyone — correct?" feeds Defence ¶2 and ¶3.
- Q17-style authoritative recital: "And the emergency-room record shows you were admitted at [time]. Correct?" forces the plaintiff to accept a timeline they cannot verify in the moment.
- Q19-style absolute: "Prior to [incident date], you had never experienced any falls of any kind — correct?" sets up impeachment via pre-existing condition records.
- Q20-style sweep: "And you've never filed any prior personal injury or disability claim — at any time, for any reason?" is the perjury-trap.

## Style markers the AI examiner should reach for

When generating questions for any phase, the model should be capable of:
- Trap words: `never`, `any`, `always`, `ever`, `exactly`
- Authoritative recital: "And the [document] shows [fact]. Correct?"
- Range-offering: "what — [reasonable range]?"
- Compound stacking: 3+ facts in one breath
- Conversational lulling: parenthetical phrasings that lower the witness's guard
- Absolute closing: "isn't that right?" / "yes?" / "correct?" pinning a one-word answer
